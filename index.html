    Optimizing Urban Water Management Through Mulch Selection

    Research Context

    Urban environments face significant challenges in managing stormwater runoff, which contributes to flooding, erosion, and water quality degradation. Mulches, as permeable soil coverings, offer a sustainable solution by intercepting and retaining water, reducing runoff, and enhancing soil moisture retention. This study explores the intrinsic water retention properties of 10 mulch types, providing critical data to optimize their use in urban green infrastructure, such as rain gardens, bioswales, and permeable pavements.

    Study Objectives

    The primary objective is to quantify the water-holding capacities of organic (e.g., Bark pine, Leaves) and inorganic (e.g., Lava, White Marble) mulches without soil interaction. By isolating mulch performance, this research informs evidence-based selection for urban applications, balancing water retention, durability, cost, and environmental impact. The study also examines how mulch mass influences retention, offering insights into optimal application rates.

    Methodology

    Ten mulch types were tested at three mass levels, corresponding to approximate thicknesses of 2 cm, 4 cm, and 6 cm, in a standardized box (area: 0.0962 mÂ²). Each mulch was saturated with water, drained, and weighed in three replicates to measure dry and wet weights. Water retention (kg) and capacity (%) were calculated to assess performance. The dataset enables comparative analysis across organic and inorganic materials, with results visualized and analyzed through this interactive dashboard.

    Key Findings

    • Organic mulches, such as Leaves (mean capacity: ~200%) and Straw (~400%), exhibit superior water retention capacities compared to inorganic mulches like Lava (~10%) and White Marble (~5%), due to their porous, fibrous structures.
    • Inorganic mulches, while less absorbent, offer exceptional durability (10+ years), making them suitable for low-maintenance urban landscapes.
    • Water retention increases with mulch mass across all types, with organic mulches showing stronger linear relationships (R-squared > 0.9) than inorganic ones (~0.5-0.7).
    • Lightweight mulches like Pine needle and Leaves provide cost-effective retention, ideal for temporary or budget-constrained projects.

    Applications and Impact

    These findings guide urban planners, landscape architects, and engineers in selecting mulches to enhance stormwater management, reduce urban heat island effects, and support sustainable landscapes. By integrating mulch performance data into green infrastructure designs, cities can mitigate flooding, improve water quality, and promote ecological resilience. This dashboard facilitates interactive exploration of the data, empowering stakeholders to make informed decisions tailored to specific urban contexts.

    Conclusion and Future Directions
    
                  
    
                  
    
                    
    Research Summary
    
                    
    This study demonstrates that organic mulches, such as Leaves, Straw, and Pine needle, significantly outperform inorganic mulches like Lava and White Marble in water retention capacity, making them ideal for urban water management applications. Inorganic mulches, however, offer unmatched durability, suitable for long-term, low-maintenance landscapes. The strong linear relationships between mulch mass and retention for organic mulches (R-squared > 0.9) provide reliable predictive models, while inorganic mulches show more variability, suggesting the need for tailored application strategies. These insights empower urban planners to optimize mulch selection for green infrastructure, balancing retention, cost, and longevity.
    
                    
    Practical Implications
    
                    
    By leveraging high-capacity organic mulches in rain gardens and bioswales, cities can reduce stormwater runoff, mitigate flooding, and enhance water quality. Inorganic mulches, with their aesthetic appeal and durability, are better suited for decorative landscapes or areas with minimal water retention needs. The interactive dashboard enables stakeholders to explore these trade-offs, tailoring mulch choices to specific urban contexts, such as high-traffic areas, water-scarce regions, or ecologically sensitive zones.

By integrating mulch performance into urban planning, cities can enhance resilience to climate change, improve ecological health, and create livable, water-efficient environments.
